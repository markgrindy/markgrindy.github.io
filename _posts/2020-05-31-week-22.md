I&apos;m selling a pair of lamps on craigslist and Samantha from the suburbs expresses interest but texts back&#x3a; &quot;I would come by today to buy them but there is a riot in St Paul.&quot; <br/><br/>Two days ago a Minneapolis police officer continued to kneel on a man&apos;s neck after he&apos;d suffocated him, had killed him. People ought to be demonstrating, and 99.9 percent will protest peacefully. It&apos;s true, though, some stores have been looted. Others have closed for the day. At the same time, it&apos;s not like there&apos;s a smoldering upturned car on every city block. You don&apos;t have to look hard to see St. Paul is not burning. I guess Samantha doesn&apos;t want to turn on that particular light. <br/><br/>Across the street from our apartment, the young white guys from the tattoo parlor are outside boarding up the windows. It&apos;s a pleasant day. Peaceful, even. Green leaves rustle in the midday sun. Cars hum and honk. My ears search for shouts and sirens but find none. <br/><br/>The tattoo parlor, normally open until midnight, has been closed for the past two months. The coronavirus lockdown is still in place, and it turns out accepting money from someone in exchange for the opportunity to hammer a wet needle into their naked skin is not an essential business. But the guys cover only the floor-to-ceiling windows with rough plywood, leaving the glass door ready to open for business the moment the lockdown is lifted. <br/><br/> One guy is light and lanky; the other practically has sandbags for arms. It&apos;s like Bert and Ernie got inked. Both wear masks. Ernie pulls out a can of black spray paint and starts shaking. <br/><br/> Sarah calls to let me know the clinic is closing for the day, due to the protests. I tell her about the muppets. Ernie may look like a linebacker, but from the instant he starts spraying it&apos;s plain to see he has the finesse of a ballerina. The artful, looping lettering reminds me of Palatino, the legibility-conscious typeface of Hermann Zapf, but with more character. With each swoop of his arm, the lines emerge clean and the message clear&#x3a; &quot;Justice for George Floyd.&quot;
